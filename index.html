Examples of Continuous and Categorical Data:

Continuous Data:

Continuous data refers to data that can take any value within a given range. It is measurable and can be represented on a scale.

  • Height (e.g., 170 cm, 5.7 ft)
  • Weight (e.g., 65 kg, 140 lbs)
  • Temperature (e.g., 22°C, 72°F)

Categorical Data:

Categorical data refers to data that can be divided into specific categories or groups that do not have a natural order.

  • Gender (e.g., Male, Female)
  • Color (e.g., Red, Blue, Green)
  • Type of Car (e.g., Sedan, SUV, Coupe)

Understanding Normal Distribution:

The normal distribution is a symmetric probability distribution characterized by a bell-shaped curve. In this distribution, most data points cluster around the mean, with fewer occurrences as you move away from the mean.

How to Assess Normality:

  • Visual Inspection: Plot a histogram to check if the data forms a bell-shaped curve, indicating normality.
  • Q-Q Plot: Use a quantile-quantile (Q-Q) plot to visually assess if the data follows a normal distribution.
  • Statistical Tests: The Shapiro-Wilk test can be used to formally test for normality. A significant result suggests the data is not normally distributed.

Understanding the Goal of Your Test:

It's important to clearly define the purpose of your analysis to choose the correct statistical test. Here are the three main categories:

  • Testing for Differences: When you want to compare groups to see if there are significant differences in their values. For example:
    • Comparing the average test scores of two different teaching methods (Two groups, continuous data).
    • Testing if three different diets lead to different weight loss outcomes (More than two groups, continuous data).
  • Testing for Relationships: When you're examining if and how two continuous variables are related to each other. For example:
    • Exploring the relationship between height and weight (Continuous data).
    • Investigating how advertising spend affects sales (Continuous data).
  • Testing for Association: When you're analyzing if two categorical variables are associated with each other, meaning one variable's categories are linked with the other’s categories. For example:
    • Testing if gender is associated with voting preference (Categorical data).
    • Examining the relationship between a customer’s preferred type of product and their region (Categorical data).
